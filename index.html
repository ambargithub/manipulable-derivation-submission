Motion of a string






Playing a guitar and walking on a rope might seem like two very different activities. But if we try moving beyond their visual appearance, we might find a striking similarity between the two.

Drag the slider to discover the similarity !!

Even though differing in scale, a guitar and a ropewalk are both perfect examples of string tied at both ends. Which is the behavior we wish to study currently.


Depending on the position of our hands(for a guitar) and body(for a ropewalk) the behavior of the string/s varies. Let us now try to interact with this string to see what affects its behavior.

Drag the horizontal slider to choose where you want to apply force on the string.

Drag the vertical slider to decide the direction and amount of force applied on the string.



We have now gained some intuition about the behavior of a string. But what is exact nature of this relationship ? In order to find that out, we need to introduce an important assumption in our derivation. We assume that our string is composed of numerous small mass points which are connected to each other by springs. The distance b/w any two mass points is dx, which tends to zero. This step is called discretization.

Drag the slider to convert this continuous string into a discrete one.

Hover over any portion of the string to observe the mass points closely.



If we can derive the behavior of either of the strings mass point, we can begin to predict the exact behavior of the string. In order to do that, lets focus on any one mass point and name it \(m_{i}\). We can pick \(m_{i}\) to be anywhere on the string.

Drag the slider to explore \(m_{i}\)'s positions












Zoom in the highlighted portion to sharpen your focus !!

As we zoom in, we find out that \(m_{i}\) is connected to two other mass points on both the sides by two springs. Lets call these mass points \(m_{i-1}\) and \(m_{i+1}\).

Hover over the mass points to locate them on the x and y axis.

You would have noticed that ever since we narrowed down to these mass points, we also began defining their coordinates. We call this a geometric structure, which is a crucial step to arrive at a mathematical model

\(m_{i}\)'s behavior is affected by the force that the stretched springs exert on it. We need to calculate this force exerted by \(m_{i-1}\) and \(m_{i+1}\) on \(m_{i}\).
But before that, lets look at a simple example to brush up our understanding of spring force. As we can see, the figure on the right shows two objects connected to each other by a spring. What would be the force exerted by the spring on the right object, if we pull it towards the right ?

Drag the object to find out.
We can see that the force is directly proportional to the stretch in the string.
\( F\) = \(\ -k \)\(( stretch\ in\ the\ spring)\)
The negative sign shows that the force is opposite to the direction of stretch.



Now lets come back to \(m_{i}\). Mass point \(m_{i}\) would experience two spring forces, due to both \(m_{i-1}\) and \(m_{i+1}\). In each cases, the force would depend upon the amount of stretch in the spring. Lets first calculate the force due to \(m_{i-1}\), which we can denote as \( F_{i-1}( x_{i})\).
          \( F_{i-1}( x_{i})\) = \( -k_{1} \)(stretch in the spring)
Drag the slider to observe the transition from a stretched string to an unstretched string.
As you can see, stretching changes the distance between the \(m_{i-1}\) & \(m_{i}\) from BC to AC. Therefore, amount of stretch
           =\( AC - BC\)
          = hypotenuse - dx
          \(= \ \sqrt{( AB)^{2} +\ ( dx)^{2}} \ -\ dx\ \)
          \(=\ AB ...........\ Assuming\ dx\ \rightarrow 0\)
          \(=\ Y_{x_{i-1}} \ -\ Y_{x_{i}}\)
Therefore, \( F_{i-1}( x_{i})\) \(=\ k_{1}( Y_{x_{i-1}} \ -\ Y_{x_{i}})\)
Similarly, \( F_{i+1}( x_{i})\) \(=\ k_{1}( Y_{x_{i+1}} \ -\ Y_{x_{i}})\)













Now that we have calculated the force acting on \(m_{i}\) due to both the mass points, lets sum it up.
          \(F_{i-1}( x_{i})\) + \(F_{i+1}( x_{i})\) \({{{=\ k_{1} \ ( Y_{( x_{i-1})}} \ -\ 2Y_{( x}}_{_{i}) \ } +\ Y_{( x_{i+1})}})\)
This is the total force acting on mass point \(m_{i}\). We can similarly calculate the force acting on the other mass points as well.
Drag the slider to see the total force acting on other masspoints on the string
Since
          \(x_{i+1}\) \(=\) \(\ x_{i} \ \) + \(dx\)      \(\&\)     \(x_{i-1}\) = \(x_{i}\) - \(dx\)
On dropping \(i\) from the equation
         \({F\ =\ Total\ force\ =\ k_{1} \ ( Y_{( x\ +\ dx) \ } -2Y_{( x)} +\ Y}_{( x\ -\ dx)}) \)
